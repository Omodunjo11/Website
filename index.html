01 About

Lagos → Bradford → New York → Atlanta → Philadelphia

My name is Onaolapo Michael Odunjo. Most people call me Lapo. I grew up in Lagos, Nigeria and have since lived, studied, and worked in Bradford, New York, Atlanta, and Philadelphia. Moving between those places shaped how I think — I learned early that the same problem looks completely different depending on where you're standing.

I work in two areas that don't usually overlap: building AI systems for regulated industries, and investing capital in African and emerging markets. On the AI side, I design systems that can be trusted — ones that know when to flag uncertainty, how to avoid compounding errors, and how to operate under institutional rules. On the investing side, I evaluate companies in markets where data is thin, regulations shift quickly, and the standard approaches don't apply.

What draws me to both is the same thing: I want to find where something is about to go wrong before it does. Whether that's an AI model quietly drifting off course, a deal with hidden regulatory risk, or a financial system built on assumptions that won't hold — I like working on problems where careful thinking early prevents serious damage later.

At Wharton, I serve as President of the Graduate Association, running governance and overseeing a multi-million-dollar budget across 1,700 students. I also founded Young Africans in Diaspora — a community I built because it needed to exist and it didn't yet.

Retrieval Systems
HAL

HAL is an AI system I built that retrieves information to answer questions — but more importantly, knows when not to answer. I built an evaluation framework that tracks how often the system gives wrong information, drifts off topic, and how accurate its answers are over time. In institutional settings, a wrong answer isn't just unhelpful — it can cause real downstream harm. HAL is designed with that in mind from the ground up.

RAG architecture Hallucination eval Drift monitoring Abstention policy Precision / recall

Anomaly & Churn Systems

At Amazon, I built machine learning models that predict when subscribers are about to cancel, across 1.17 million users and 2 billion+ records. 05 Capital Allocation & Infrastructure
Growth Equity & Secondaries · City Ventures
African & Emerging Market Investing

City Ventures is a private markets firm investing in African and emerging markets — fintech, financial infrastructure, and essential services. My role covers the full investment process: I build models to understand how large these markets are and how they'll grow, evaluate companies on financial health and risk, lead deals from initial review through to closing, and monitor companies after investment.

I have reviewed 60+ companies, led two closed investments (both in the mid-to-high seven figures), and built the firm's framework for evaluating deals in markets where the standard U.S. playbook doesn't apply — including stress-testing for currency swings, regulatory disruption, and exit uncertainty across Nigeria, Kenya, South Africa, Ghana, and Egypt.

Infrastructure Modeling
Energy Infrastructure Projects

Two infrastructure projects where my engineering background directly improved the investment analysis:

AquaGrid Energy Solutions: I built a 10–20 year financial model for a hydropower mini-grid in Africa, including taxes, depreciation, currency risk, and tariff sensitivity. Because I understood the engineering, I could assess whether the performance assumptions were realistic — not just whether the numbers looked good on paper.

IslandGrid Energy Solutions: I developed a hybrid energy model (hydro, solar, and wind) that accounted for climate risk, disaster scenarios, and different financing options. The goal was to find the most resilient capital structure, not just the most optimistic one.

The research sits at the intersection of electrochemical engineering and machine learning — two fields I've worked in on opposite ends of my career.
